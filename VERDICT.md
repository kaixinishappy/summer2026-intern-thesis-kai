# FinTech Disruption of Traditional Banking

**Thesis question:** Digital payment platforms, neobanks, and embedded finance players have taken significant market share from legacy institutions. Thesis evaluate whether FinTech disruption has run its course or whether the second wave of AI-native financial services is just beginning

## Verdict

The **first wave of fintech** has largely run its course. Between 2010 and 2021, payment platforms, neobanks, and challengers like PayPal, Block, and Robinhood won on user experience, not a fundamentally different banking model. Many turned unprofitable after 2021 while rising rates strengthened bank earnings, and traditional institutions have since reclaimed the pricing power and market position fintech briefly took. This is backed by price and profitability data across multiple years, and corroborated by a broader fintech-sector-ETF-vs-bank-sector-ETF check showing the same rise-and-rollover shape beyond the 7 tickers picked here.

The **second wave, AI-native finance**, can't be evaluated with the same confidence. Most AI-first firms are private or too newly public for meaningful data, so there's no way yet to tell lasting advantage from early hype. Search interest and AI-related filings show only modest upticks starting 2024. That thinness is itself the finding: too early to call, not absent.

Taken together, **incumbent banks look better positioned to absorb AI than to be displaced by it.** The "banks are too slow" narrative is plausible but has little empirical support here: the limited evidence shows banks adopting and disclosing AI at least as actively as challengers.

The project's main empirical contribution is separating these two waves rather than treating fintech disruption as one continuous story. Wave 1 and Wave 2 run on different dynamics and different timelines, and blending them risks misattributing what's actually happening.

## Bottom line

**Fintech didn't die, and AI hasn't clearly taken over anything yet.** By the measures available here (price, profitability, search attention), the first wave has been substantially reabsorbed by the banks it once threatened, most visible in the 2022 net-income split where challengers posted losses the same year bank profits grew, and confirmed at the broader sector level (FINX vs. KBWB) so it isn't just the 7 tickers picked. The second wave is too early to call with market data, since the relevant companies aren't public; what thin evidence exists (EDGAR `agentic` language, search interest) is consistent with a real, early inflection starting around 2024, not nothing happening. The smart bet, though not something this project can prove, is that banks capture the AI wave the same way they captured the first one: they have the profits to fund it, and, in this small sample, their names are the ones on the filings that mention it.
